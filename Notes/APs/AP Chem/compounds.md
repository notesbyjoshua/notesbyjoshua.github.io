# Unit 2: Compound Structure and Properties

## Types of Bonds (ADD BOND STRENGTH)

### Ionic Bonds

Ionic bonds are bonds between a metal and non-metal, and involve the anion taking an electron from the cation. For example, in the ionic bond between $$N$$ and $$Cl$$, $$Cl$$ takes an electron to be $$Cl^-$$, and as a result $$Na$$ becomes $$Na^+$$.

### Covalent Bonds

Covalent bonds are bonds between two non-metals, and involve the anion taking an electron from the cation.

### Metallic

Metallic bonds are bonds between two metals, and are different from ionic and covalent bonds in the fact that the electrons are delocalized in a metallic bond. This means that electrons hop from one metal atom to the next, forming a "sea of electrons," where the electrons are distributed throughout the bond rather than being set in one place, making them delocalized.

## Nomenclature

### Molecular compound nomenclature

If a compound is made up entirely out of covalent bonds, it is an molecular compound. To name a binary molecular compound (made up of two non-metals (e.g. $$B_3$$ is Triboron). Next, state the next non-metal/polyatomic ion in ion form. If the second element is not a polyatomic ion, add an *-ide* suffix to it (e.g. $$O^(2-)$$ becomes oxide). Note that molecular compounds that are acids (usually containing hydrogen) have naming conventions described in [Unit 8]({{ '/notes/ap/chem/acidbase/' | relative_url }}).

### Ionic compound nomenclature

If a compound is made up entirely out of ionic bonds, it is an ionic compound. To name an ionic compound, you start with the name of the cation (the metal), and write in parenthesis its charge if it has a variable charge (e.g. $$Fe^3+$$ becomes iron (III)). Next, write the name of the anion in ion form (e.g. $$F^-$$ becomes fluoride). Note that hydrogen ($$H^+$$) does not act like a metal but ammonium ($$NH_4^+$$) does act like a metal. Prefixes are not needed.

## Drawing 2D Molecules

### Octet Rule

The Octet Rule states that in a compound, all atoms much have a full octet, or have 8 valence electrons. There are some notable exceptions:

1. Fewer than 8 electrons: hydrogen ($$H$$) and helium ($$He$$) both only need 2 electrons for a full octet (and can only hold a maximum of 2 electrons). 2. More than 8 electrons: All of the elements in the square from phosphorous ($$P$$) to astantine ($$At$$) can have expanded octets, where they can hold more than 8 electrons in their shell.

### Lewis Structures

Lewis structures are very compact ways to draw 2D molecular structure. Please note that this is not completely accurate to the molecule in real life since this is just a 2D rendition of a 3D model.

Steps:
1. Count the total number of valence electrons  
2. Choose a central atom. If there are only two elements inside the compound, there is no central atom (just draw the two side by side). Usually, the central atom comes first (unless it is hydrogen) in the representation of the compound. The central atom is always the least electronegative atom.
3. Start with single bonds between atoms. Then, fill out the octets of each atom, so that each will have a complete one.
4. Count the number of valence electrons that are present within the compound. A bond counts as 2 electrons.
5. If there are too many electrons, add another bond (there should be a maximum of 3 bonds between two atoms), and if there are too few electrons, put extra electrons on the central atom. Make sure to keep in mind the exceptions to the octet rule when doing this step.
6. Repeat steps 4 and 5 until you have a valid structure.
7. There could be many valid structures. To pick the correct one, choose the structure with the least formal charge.
8. If there are resonance structures, make sure to indicate them.

## VESPR Theory

VESPR (EXPAND) Theory explains how molecules are structured in 3D. Basically, electrons in lone pair/bonds will always try to repel each other, so the most favorable configuration is the one where the electrons are the most spread out, meaning the potential energy is the lowest (refer to [Unit 1]({{ '/notes/ap/chem/atomicstrucprop/' | relative_url }}) for the definition of electric potential energy).

## Hybridization

VESPR Theory predicts that carbon can only form 2 bonds, but it is determined that it can hold up to 4! So what is going on? Turns out, when two atoms make bonds, their orbitals mix and hybridize, creating hybrid orbitals.

- sp → linear  
- sp² → trigonal planar  
- sp³ → tetrahedral
