# Unit 6: Thermochemistry

---

# 1. The Thermodynamic Universe

Definition of a Thermodynamic Universe: For simplicity, we can assume that our universe is made up of two parts: the system and the surroundings. We also assume that our universe is a closed system, where no energy/heat or matter enters or leaves  

### Key Idea
- System → what we are studying  
- Surroundings → everything else  

---

# 2. Heat (q)

Heat (q): Energy transferred between objects due to a temperature difference, and is measured from the SURROUNDING’s point of view  

- Always flows from hot to cold (High q to Low q) (2nd Law of Thermodynamics)  

## Heat Equation

$$
q = mc\Delta T
$$

- \( m \) = mass  
- \( c \) = specific heat capacity  
- \( \Delta T \) = temperature change  

## Non-Ideal Calorimeter

$$
q = m s \Delta T + c \Delta T
$$

- \( s \) = specific heat  
- \( c \) = calorimeter constant  

## System vs Surroundings

$$
q_{\text{sys}} = -q_{\text{surr}}
$$

(if no subscript given, we always assume \( q = q_{\text{sys}} \))  

---

# 3. Specific Heat Capacity

Specific heat capacity (c): Amount of heat needed to raise 1g of a substance 1°C  

$$
c_{\text{water}} = 4.184 \, \frac{J}{g^\circ C}
$$

---

# 4. Energy

Energy: Capacity to do work or produce heat (units: calories or joules)  

$$
E_{\text{universe}} = 0
$$

(Energy can’t be created/destroyed)

---

# 5. Kinetic Energy

Kinetic Energy: Energy of motion  

$$
KE = \frac{1}{2}mv^2
$$

---

# 6. Temperature and Kinetic Energy

Temperature: Temperature is the average kinetic energy of a collection of molecules  

## Relationship

$$
KE_{\text{avg}} = \frac{3}{2}RT
$$

Per molecule:

$$
KE_{\text{avg}} = \frac{3}{2}k_B T
$$

$$
k_B = 1.38 \times 10^{-23} \, \frac{J}{K}
$$

---

# 7. Maxwell-Boltzmann Distributions

Maxwell-Boltzmann Diagrams: These diagrams show the range of velocities for different gases  

### Key Observations

- Gases have a range of speeds  
- Lighter gases → higher average speed  
- Can also be plotted vs kinetic energy  

Used for:
- Activation energy analysis  
- Reaction rate comparisons  

---

# 8. Potential Energy and Internal Energy

Potential Energy: Energy due to position or molecular composition  

For a thermodynamic system:

$$
U = q + W
$$

---

# 9. Work

Work done by a System:

$$
W = PV
$$

---

# 10. State Functions

State function: Only depends on initial and final states, not the path  

Examples:
- Pressure  
- Volume  
- Temperature  
- Enthalpy (\( H \))  
- Internal Energy (\( U \))  
- Gibbs Free Energy  
- Entropy (\( S \))  

---

# 11. Enthalpy

Enthalpy: The amount of heat that is gained or lost measured from the SYSTEM’s point of view (opposite of heat)  

## Relationships

$$
H = q
$$

$$
H = U + PV
$$

---

# 12. Endothermic vs Exothermic Reactions

## Exothermic Reaction

In an endothermic reaction, the system loses heat and the surroundings gain heat  

- Enthalpy is negative  
- \( q_{\text{sys}} \) negative  
- \( q_{\text{surr}} \) positive  

---

## Endothermic Reaction

In an endothermic reaction, the system absorbs heat while the surroundings lose heat  

- Enthalpy is positive  
- \( q_{\text{sys}} \) positive  
- \( q_{\text{surr}} \) negative  

---

# 13. Hess’s Law

Hess’s Law: States that the total enthalpy change for a chemical reaction is the same no matter how many steps the reaction is carried out in  

### Rules

- Reverse reaction → flip sign of \( \Delta H \)  
- Multiply reaction → multiply \( \Delta H \)  

---

# 14. Enthalpy of Formation

Standard Enthalpy of Formation (\( \Delta H \)) (for a naturally occuring element) is always 0  

## Reaction Enthalpy

$$
\Delta H_{\text{reaction}} = \Delta H_{\text{products}} - \Delta H_{\text{reactants}}
$$

---

## Solution Formation Insight

If \( \Delta H_{\text{solution}} \) is slightly endothermic, the solution could form due to entropy (\( S \))  

If \( \Delta H_{\text{solution}} \) is highly endothermic, solution most likely will not form  

---

# 15. Bond Energy

Bond Energy: Breaking bonds requires energy input, forming bonds releases energy  

---

## Bond Enthalpy

Bond enthalpy: Energy needed to break a bond in the gas phase  

## Reaction Estimate

$$
\Delta H_{\text{reaction}} = \text{bonds broken} - \text{bonds formed}
$$

### Notes

- Does not take into account environmental factors  
- Least reliable because it is an average  

---

# 16. Clausius-Clapeyron Equation

Clausius-Clapeyron Equation:

$$
\ln\left(\frac{P_2}{P_1}\right) = -\frac{\Delta H_{\text{vap}}}{R} \left(\frac{1}{T_2} - \frac{1}{T_1}\right)
$$
