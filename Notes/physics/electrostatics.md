# Electrostatics (This is just a test)

## Electric Charge

Electric charge is a fundamental property of matter. Protons carry positive charge, electrons carry negative charge, and both have magnitude:

$$
e = 1.60 \times 10^{-19} \, \text{C}
$$

- Charge is transferred through the movement of electrons  
- Positive charge = electron deficiency  
- Negative charge = electron surplus  

### Quantization of Charge

$$
q = ne, \quad n \in \mathbb{Z}
$$

---

## Conservation of Charge

The total charge in an isolated system remains constant. Charge cannot be created or destroyed, only transferred.

---

## Grounding

Grounding connects an object to the Earth, allowing excess charge to flow away and neutralize the object.

---

## Electroscopes

Electroscopes detect charge through the separation of conducting leaves.

### Charging by Conduction
- Touch a charged object to the electroscope  
- Charge transfers  
- Both objects end up with the same sign  

### Charging by Induction
1. Bring charged object near electroscope  
2. Ground the electroscope  
3. Remove ground, then remove object  

Result: the electroscope gains opposite charge  

---

## Polarization

A nearby charged object causes charges in a neutral object to rearrange, resulting in attraction.

---

## Coulomb's Law

The force between two point charges is:

$$
\vec{F} = k \frac{q_1 q_2}{r^2} \hat{r}
$$

$$
k = 8.99 \times 10^9 \, \text{N} \cdot \text{m}^2 / \text{C}^2
$$

- Like charges repel  
- Opposite charges attract  
- Force is a vector  

### Permittivity of Free Space

$$
k = \frac{1}{4\pi \varepsilon_0}
$$

$$
\varepsilon_0 = 8.85 \times 10^{-12} \, \frac{\text{C}^2}{\text{N} \cdot \text{m}^2}
$$

---

## Superposition Principle

The net force on a charge is the vector sum of all individual forces acting on it.

---

## Electric Field

The electric field is defined as:

$$
\vec{E} = \frac{\vec{F}}{q}
$$

For a point charge:

$$
\vec{E} = \frac{1}{4\pi \varepsilon_0} \frac{Q}{r^2} \hat{r}
$$

- Direction is defined by a positive test charge  
- Independent of velocity (non-relativistic)  

For continuous charge distributions:

$$
\vec{E} = k \int \frac{dq}{r^2}
$$

---

## Electric Field Lines

- Start at positive charges  
- End at negative charges  
- Represent direction and strength of the field  

---

## Gauss's Law

### Charge Densities

$$
\lambda = \frac{q}{L}, \quad \sigma = \frac{q}{A}, \quad \rho = \frac{q}{V}
$$

---

### Electric Flux

$$
\Phi_E = \vec{E} \cdot \vec{A} = EA \cos \theta
$$

For closed surfaces:

$$
\Phi_E = \oint \vec{E} \cdot d\vec{A} = \frac{Q_{\text{enc}}}{\varepsilon_0}
$$

---

### Gaussian Surface

An imaginary closed surface used to apply Gauss's Law (typically spheres or cylinders).

---

## Electric Potential / Voltage

Electric potential is the work done per unit charge:

$$
V = \frac{\Delta U}{q}
$$

For a point charge:

$$
V = \frac{kQ}{r}
$$

---

### Work and Energy

$$
W = Vq
$$

$$
\Delta U = \frac{kQq}{r}
$$

---

### Potential from Electric Field

$$
V = -\int \vec{E} \cdot d\vec{r}
$$

---

## Equipotential Lines

- Points with equal electric potential  
- Always perpendicular to electric field lines  

---

## Electron Volt

A unit of energy:

$$
1 \, \text{eV} = 1.6 \times 10^{-19} \, \text{J}
$$

- Represents energy gained by an electron moving through a potential difference of 1 V
